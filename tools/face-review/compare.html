What each chrono can show — and what it can't

A watch complication comes in eight shapes. Only two of them are a number with a range — and those are the only two a needle can point at. The other six are words or pictures, which cannot go on these dials without printing something your design does not have. So the three chronos now offer only the two that fit, and the watch's own editor will not even list the rest.

The three dials, and what each one is for

Your design already says what each dial is — the engraved numbers and the little icon are the label. Each keeps its own scale, its own needle and its own arc, permanently. Two things change when you set a complication: which number the needle follows, and the small icon, which becomes the icon of whatever you set — so you can always tell what the needle means.

Fixed properly this time: the icons now match your engraved marks

Twice wrong before this. First I sized them off a padded box the code uses for collision testing, so they came out about double and the battery one printed over the “50”. Then I over-corrected and shrank them — your call, they were far too small. Both times I was reading the icon's path coordinates instead of looking at what the design actually draws.

So I measured it: each of your engraved glyphs is now rendered through the same engine that bakes the dial and its ink measured. Your heart is 8×7.5, your footprints 6.4×7.1, your bolt 5.7×8.5 — and a provider's icon is now given a box that matches that presence, so it reads at the same weight as the mark it replaces. The bolt's box is the one that would land on the “50” (your bolt tapers to a thin tip; a solid icon doesn't), so on that dial only, the icon slides 1.9 units up toward the centre — into empty plate — and your engraved bolt itself never moves.

The needle reads the numbers that are actually on the dial

If what you assign fits the engraved scale — a pulse on the 0–250 dial, a battery percentage on the 0–100 dial — the needle points at the real value: 72 beats sits on 72. If it does not fit, like 415 calories on a dial that stops at 100, the needle falls back to showing how full it is, which is the only honest thing that dial can say. I found this by zooming into the prototype: a resting pulse was pointing at “50”, because the needle was following the provider's own range instead of the numbers milled into the plate.

Only two things ever change inside a chrono

The plate, both sets of tick marks and every engraved number are permanent — they are part of the dial, not the complication, so nothing can wipe them. A complication may move the needle (and fill the arc, on the two dials that have one), and it may put its own icon in the small spot where your heart, bolt and footprints sit. Nothing else. No value is ever printed. Clear a dial and your own engraved icon comes straight back.
